*I speak, and through me sings Y'ffre. My tales are echoes and echoes of songs He once sang, of threads woven by his throat into tapestries ingrained in the minds of we faithful. The calls of birds, twinkles of stars, and lap of the sea live within me.*
—Spinner Girnalin<sup>[[1]](#cite_note-Girnalin-1)</sup> Girnalin was one of the revered [Spinners](Lore_Spinner.md),<sup>[[1]](#cite_note-Girnalin-1)</sup> priestly lorekeepers and [magical](Lore_Magic.md) storytellers among the [Bosmer](Lore_Bosmer.md) devoted to [Y'ffre](Lore_Y%27ffre.md) and preservation of Bosmeri culture, history and laws through metaphors and naratives.<sup>[[2]](#cite_note-SAS-2)</sup><sup>[[3]](#cite_note-TSOY-3)</sup> She was particularly knowledgeable in various aspects of spiritual metaphysics,<sup>[[1]](#cite_note-Girnalin-1)</sup> including the [Third Walking Way](Lore_Walking_Ways.md#Third_Walking_Way_-_The_Prolix_Tower),<sup>[[1]](#cite_note-Girnalin-1)</sup><sup>[[4]](#cite_note-LOV-4)</sup> as well as the history of the [Dwemer](Lore_Dwemer.md).<sup>[[1]](#cite_note-Girnalin-1)</sup>

## History
[![](https://images.uesp.net/thumb/b/bf/ON-place-Elden_Root.jpg/200px-ON-place-Elden_Root.jpg)](https://en.uesp.net/wiki/File:ON-place-Elden_Root.jpg) [](https://en.uesp.net/wiki/File:ON-place-Elden_Root.jpg) Elden Root *Strange wanderer, wandering wayfarer, roaming vagabond of adventure, hail. I invite you, sit a spell and join me.
Perhaps I shall spin a tale for you, perhaps not. It all depends upon your temperament, doesn't it? To listen or to not.*
—Spinner Girnalin<sup>[[1]](#cite_note-Girnalin-1)</sup> Little is known of Girnalin's early life. She pursued spiritual study and eventually became a priestess of [Y'ffre](Lore_Y%27ffre.md) and a Spinner. By [2E 582](Lore_Second_Era.md#2E_582), she was living in [Elden Root](Lore_Elden_Root.md), where she welcomed passing travelers to sit with her, hear her tales, and take part in philosophical discussion. At that time, she was known to speak on at least three major themes: the Sky, the Sea, and [Aetherius](Lore_Aetherius.md), and how each was bound to Y'ffre.<sup>[[1]](#cite_note-Girnalin-1)</sup>

## Songs
[![](https://images.uesp.net/thumb/e/ee/ON-npc-Girnalin.jpg/200px-ON-npc-Girnalin.jpg)](https://en.uesp.net/wiki/File:ON-npc-Girnalin.jpg) [](https://en.uesp.net/wiki/File:ON-npc-Girnalin.jpg) Girnalin sitting in Elden Root, beckoning wanderers to join her and listen *I do not spin in terms of "whats" but rather in "whens." The Now of the songs that fill me consider the seas and skies, and that sea and sky beyond those of Mundus—Aetherius.
So I may speak of any of these concerns—for now*
—Spinner Girnalin<sup>[[1]](#cite_note-Girnalin-1)</sup> At least three of Girnalin's many stories are known. The following were shared with a traveler in Elden Root around 2E 582.<sup>[[1]](#cite_note-Girnalin-1)</sup>

### Story of the Sea
[![](https://images.uesp.net/thumb/6/6f/OBR-place-Abecean_Sea.jpg/200px-OBR-place-Abecean_Sea.jpg)](https://en.uesp.net/wiki/File:OBR-place-Abecean_Sea.jpg) [](https://en.uesp.net/wiki/File:OBR-place-Abecean_Sea.jpg) The Sea west of Tamriel In her Sea story, Girnalin explained that *Y'ffre taught the [birds](Lore_Birds.md) to sing and the waves to lap against the beach. Through Y'ffre's song, he also gained sight-perception of all that occurs within the sight of birds and the reflection of waters, and he echoes their songs and crashing waves*; when she was asked whether anything could exist beyond such perception, she replied, *Of course. He sees and hears nothing, for Y'ffre is now the [Earth-Bones](Lore_Earth_Bones.md), the frame upon which Nature is laid and woven. His sight-perception and song-echo instead are loomed through his bones and woven into tapestries of song for we mortals to study.* Turning then to the place of the Sea within this design, she taught, *The Sea is all-enclosing, the water that encircles all of Mundus. The birds call out that which occurs over land to the sea, which reflects and echoes that song. Through study of the Sea's mystery it is possible to interpret Y'ffre's song-tapestry.* When pressed on how mortals might undertake such study, Girnalin reminded her listeners, *You already do, as do all mortals. Your frame of perception of the world is your own Bones, akin to the Earth-Bones. It is as possible to see into your own future and world as it is to immerse the Self in hysteria with no fear.* Finally, clarifying what this meant in practice, she concluded, *To sing a law, and then Speak into the heart of that law, convincing it of a subtle error and how it must change its own Self. That is how Nature's course—its own Sea—is shaped and reshaped over time. Such changes can affect the whole of Mundus.*<sup>[[1]](#cite_note-Girnalin-1)</sup>

### Story of the Sky
[![](https://images.uesp.net/thumb/c/ce/ON-sky-Moons.jpg/200px-ON-sky-Moons.jpg)](https://en.uesp.net/wiki/File:ON-sky-Moons.jpg) [](https://en.uesp.net/wiki/File:ON-sky-Moons.jpg) The Sky above Tamriel In another of her teachings, Girnalin spoke of the relationship between sky and sea, saying, *The sky mirrors the sea, and the sea also reflects its mirror. Day, night, and the places between are the realm of the sky, as Y'ffre interpreted the time-law [Anui-el](Lore_Anuiel.md) established within [Nirn](Lore_Nirn.md). Thus, the sky bridges time and nature, and measures both*. When asked to clarify the meaning of her words, she explained, *Within each half of the sphere of reflection lies half of the potentialities of Nirn's way in any given moment. Each instant is a sea-state or a sky-state. Each individual sees one or its other as weal or woe, and each moment can be turned on its side.* Pressed further on what such a turning achieves, she continued using the history of [Clan Rourken](Lore_Clan_Rourken.md) as an example, *Each moment so turned becomes an island in your path. For example, the Dwemer, united, had their way in [Dwemeris](Lore_Dwemeris.md). But one clan—Rourken, the wayward clan—struck anchor and sailed across the sky. Why do this? Mired in a sea, they sought the sky*. Finally, when the morality of creation of such islands was questioned, Girnalin affirmed and touched upon the topic of the Walking Ways, *Yes. Change is wrought in Nirn in six ways, and the turning of islands is one of these, often described by others in other terms. Of course, it is possible to acknowledge these ways without understanding them—to spin them is all I can do.*<sup>[[1]](#cite_note-Girnalin-1)</sup>

### Story of the Aetherius
[![](https://images.uesp.net/thumb/0/06/LG-quest-Prologue_01.jpg/200px-LG-quest-Prologue_01.jpg)](https://en.uesp.net/wiki/File:LG-quest-Prologue_01.jpg) [](https://en.uesp.net/wiki/File:LG-quest-Prologue_01.jpg) The cosmic mass within or encompassing the Aurbis In her story on Aetherius, Girnalin described it as *the sea of light, the Immortal Plane, the origin of magic.* She further affirmed that *Y'ffre sings not of Aetherius, but to it, weaving a song so beautiful that stars were compelled to dance and sway. They still wink and blink in memory of that song.* When the nature of the stars was raised, she clarified, *They are Aetherius. Rather, the sun and stars are holes pierced in the wall of night by [Magnus](Lore_Magnus.md) —and [other spirits](Lore_Magna_Ge.md) —seeking to escape Nirn. These holes permit Aetherius' light to enter our world. From theirs to ours does Magicka trickle.* Addressing how these lights move and flicker across the firmament, she added, *Well, consider this. As [Oblivion](Lore_Oblivion.md) is a realm composed of realms, so, too, is Aetherius a bright sea with many realms within. Each of these stars is a window into these realms, and as these realms move, so too do they move. Or close, like doors.* Finally, when pressed on the nature of those realms themselves, Girnalin concluded, *Now, that is an enormous question that I cannot answer. Many [souls](Lore_Souls.md) of mortals become spirits of another sort in that place—and that is all I can say with any certainty. As difficult as it is to travel to Oblivion, it is far more to go to Aetherius.*<sup>[[1]](#cite_note-Girnalin-1)</sup>
