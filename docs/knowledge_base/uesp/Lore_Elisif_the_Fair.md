Elisif the Fair was the beloved wife of [Torygg](Lore_Torygg.md), High King of [Skyrim](Lore_Skyrim.md), before his death at the beginning of the [Stormcloak Rebellion](Lore_Stormcloak_Rebellion.md). Following Torygg's death, she took his place as Jarl of [Solitude](Lore_Solitude.md), though the decision on who would assume the role of High King (or Queen) was delayed due to the outbreak of the rebellion. Elisif believed that the throne of Skyrim was her right, as High King Torygg's widow. However, she believed it best not to formally announce her intentions until the rebellion was quashed. She was a popular leader among her people, although some of her courtiers had concerns about her young age and inexperience, and believed she was under the thumb of [General Tullius](https://en.uesp.net/wiki/Lore:General_Tullius).<sup>[[1]](#cite_note-Skyrim-1)</sup>

Like her husband, she supported the [Empire](Lore_Empire.md), and in turn enjoyed the Empire's support as claimant to the throne of Skyrim. Elisif is politically savvy enough to realize that her rule, and likely her life, are in place only so long as she is useful to the Empire.<sup>[[2]](#cite_note-TESVSPOGG-2)</sup> While her husband privately worshiped [Talos](Lore_Talos.md) in defiance of the [White-Gold Concordat](Lore_White-Gold_Concordat.md), Elisif herself denied being a Talos worshipper, but nonetheless requested the [Last Dragonborn](Lore_Last_Dragonborn.md) to place her husband's war horn as an offering at a Shrine of Talos, purportedly because it's what he would have wanted.<sup>[[3]](#cite_note-ET-3)</sup><sup>[[4]](#cite_note-ETF-4)</sup>

In [4E 201](Lore_Fourth_Era.md#4E_201), Elisif ordered the discontinuation of the annual festival held by the [Bards College](Lore_Bards_College.md) known as the Burning of [King Olaf](Lore_Olaf_One-Eye.md), as she thought it in poor taste, in light of her husband's death. However, when the Dragonborn rediscovered the lost [King Olaf's Verse](Lore_King_Olaf%27s_Verse.md) and it was performed for her, she recognized the festival as a righteous celebration of triumph over tyranny and allowed it to continue.<sup>[[5]](#cite_note-SRTending-5)</sup>
