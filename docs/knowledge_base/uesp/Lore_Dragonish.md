# Dragon Language

[![](https://images.uesp.net/thumb/6/6b/SR-concept-6877546391.jpg/200px-SR-concept-6877546391.jpg)](https://en.uesp.net/wiki/File:SR-concept-6877546391.jpg) [](https://en.uesp.net/wiki/File:SR-concept-6877546391.jpg) A wall engraved with dragon language The ancient **Dragon Language** (also called **Dragon tongue**,<sup>[[1]](#cite_note-Arngeir-1)</sup><sup>[[2]](#cite_note-Jawanan-2)</sup> **dragon-tongue**,<sup>[[3]](#cite_note-Tharstan-3)</sup> or **Draconic language**)<sup>[[4]](#cite_note-4)</sup> is the language used by [dragons](Lore_Dragon.md). Text in the Dragon Language is found on [Word Walls](Lore_Word_Walls.md) in ancient dungeons and on burial grounds.<sup>[[5]](#cite_note-DL-5)</sup> The runes used for writing the language are described in the [Dragon Alphabet](Lore_Dragon_Alphabet.md) article.

With sufficient understanding, certain words can be incanted to produce magical effects such as conjuring fire, frost, or wind, slowing time, or pushing an enemy away. This ability is known as the [Thu'um](Lore_Thu%27um.md) and is an inborn talent of Dragons and [Dragonborn](Lore_Dragonborn.md).<sup>[[6]](#cite_note-Skyrim-6)</sup>

Scholars posit the [Dragonlings](Lore_Dragonling.md) found in northwestern [Tamriel](Lore_Tamriel.md) are unrelated to true Dragons,<sup>[[7]](#cite_note-TBD-7)</sup> yet other sources claim they are kin.<sup>[[8]](#cite_note-Histpry DF-8)</sup><sup>[[9]](#cite_note-9)</sup> The former have their own language known as **Dragonish**,<sup>[[10]](#cite_note-DFDragonish-10)</sup> though the relation, if any, of their language to the Dragon Language spoken by true Dragons is unknown.

## Grammar
The language is structurally similar to English, with three exceptions: it has no equivalent to English's apostrophes, it tends to place prepositional phrases earlier in sentences, and verb tense is often ignored.<sup>[[UOL 1]](#cite_note-GI-11)</sup> To make a word plural, the last letter of the singular word is repeated and followed by an "e". For example, the plural form of "kel" (an Elder Scroll) is "kelle".

The singular possessive ("my", "your", "his", or "her") is formed by adding the letter "i" to the end of the word, except when the word ends in "ii", in which case an "l" is appended. The first-person plural possessive ("our") is formed by adding the letter "u". To indicate a perfect verb form (as in *"has **silenced**"*), the letters "aan" are added to the end of the word.

The language makes heavy use of compound words, and tends to be head-initial (i.e. the focus, or what a phrase is ultimately about, comes first) in most cases. For example, "Zeymahzin" (Companion) consists of the words "Zeymah" (Brother) and "Zin" (Honor) and should be understood as "brothers with honor" rather than "the honor of/between brothers".

According to at the time Senior Designer [Emil Pagliarulo](https://en.uesp.net/wiki/General:Emil_Pagliarulo), "Every shout is made up of one to three single-syllable words. Each word also functions differently depending on how many total words are present. If you just have ‘Fus,’ it’s exhaled strongly, indicating the forceful nature of the command. But if you have two or more words, ‘Fus’ is actually inhaled, as it serves to modify the second and third words, which are forcefully exhaled. That was very important to the language when used as shouts – it had to feel forceful. The same kind of force a dragon would use when spitting fire"<sup>[[UOL 2]](#cite_note-BN-12)</sup>

## Names
Dragon names are formed of three monosyllabic words that, as a whole, may be rendered as a Shout.<sup>[[36]](#cite_note-Esbern-38)</sup> Each dragon name is unique, and so dragons are able to use these Shouts to address each other across vast distances.

While some dragon names can be definitively separated into three words, others can, interestingly enough, be translated as either three or two words. For instance, "Sahrotaar" can translate to either "Sah-Rot-Aar" (Phantom-Word-Servant/Slave) or "Sahrot-Aar" (Mighty-Servant/Slave). However, as mentioned above, dragon names are said to always be made up of three words, so one should always assume that the three-word translation is correct.

Dragon names can be translated into phrases, such as the name *Odahviing* (literally: Snow-Hunter-Wing), which was converted into a coherent phrase; "*Winged Snow-Hunter*".<sup>[[36]](#cite_note-Esbern-38)</sup> If other names were translated in the same way, it can be extrapolated that *Boziikkodstrun* would be "*Bold Storm-Wielder*" or that *Sahloknir* would be "*Phantom Sky-Hunter*".

There is also the case where a mortal can be granted a *Dovahzin* (Dragon Name), by a dragon who believes they have earned it and it is considered a great honor. Two examples of these are [Ahkahtuz](Lore_The_Vestige.md) (Hunter-Pride-Blade) and [Toshrakhat](Lore_Ja%27darri.md).<sup>[[26]](#cite_note-ESONahfahlaar-28)</sup> Dragon Priests also usually have names in the dragon language, although often only two syllables long instead of three.

## Notes
- Nordic ruins were inspired by the pyramids of [Ancient Egypt](https://www.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Egypt) as magnificent stone structures that would stand the test of time. [Adam Adamowicz](https://en.uesp.net/wiki/General:Adam_Adamowicz) came up with having them shaped like ribcages, giving the developers the sense that they were made out of dead dragon skeletons. They were filled with iconography similar to [Egyptian heiroglyphs](https://www.wikipedia.org/wiki/Egyptian_hieroglyphs).<sup>[[37]](#cite_note-Reliving Skyrim-39)</sup> The script resembles scratch marks, which was inspired by [Cuneiform](https://www.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cuneiform).<sup>[[UOL 2]](#cite_note-BN-12)</sup>
- Its lexicon and vocabulary was derived from languages like Swedish, Danish and German and especially Old English according to [Emil Pagliarulo](https://en.uesp.net/wiki/General:Emil_Pagliarulo). Inspiration for this came from an Old English recording of Beowulf as a creative reference for the Dragon language.
- The idea for turning the Dragon’s words into powerful abilities came from David Lynch’s 1984 film adaptation of Dune. A fighting style called the "Weirding Way" essentially weaponizes sound, and the pattern of inhaling and exhaling directly influenced the way Dragon shouts function.<sup>[[UOL 2]](#cite_note-BN-12)</sup>
