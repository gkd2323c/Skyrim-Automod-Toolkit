# Dwemeris

**Dwemeris**,<sup>[[1]](#cite_note-DIVII-1)</sup> also known as **Old Dwarvish**<sup>[[2]](#cite_note-FE6-2)</sup> or the **Dwemer language**,<sup>[[3]](#cite_note-BD-3)</sup> is the language once spoken by the [Dwemer](Lore_Dwemer.md). This article is an attempt to summarize all that is known about it.

The Dwemer language is largely untranslatable, though the alphabet is known, and short inscriptions can often be deciphered from context. Many Dwemer documents, especially records dating from after the founding of Resdayn, were written in [Aldmeris](Lore_Ehlnofex_Languages.md), which is very close to modern Elven languages and makes them accessible to modern scholars.<sup>[[3]](#cite_note-BD-3)</sup><sup>[[4]](#cite_note-MWHasphat-4)</sup> Pronunciation of Dwemer words is not agreed upon by "learned" scholars.<sup>[[5]](#cite_note-Ht PDW-5)</sup> The letters in the written language have been characterized as "fierce strokes".<sup>[[6]](#cite_note-ONRazak-6)</sup>

As the books and other artifacts in Dwemer ruins rarely show signs of wear or age, it is thought that the Dwemer knew of a preservative effect, or an ancient device that continues to function, which denies or controls the Earth Bones governing time and decay.<sup>[[3]](#cite_note-BD-3)</sup> Imperial scholars sought to translate it through books written in Aldmeris,<sup>[[7]](#cite_note-MWExpedition-7)</sup><sup>[[8]](#cite_note-MWMystery-8)</sup> and [Divayth Fyr](Lore_Divayth_Fyr.md) worked on negotiating with the council to release the Dwemer speech to the public.<sup>[[UOL 1]](#cite_note-SSLW-9)</sup>

## Overview
In comparison to the [language of the Ayleids](Lore_Ayleidoon.md), very little is known about the language of the Dwemer. Most of it survives as names of various [Dwemer ruins](Lore_Dwemer_Ruins.md), often constructed from a limited number of roots connected together (for example *Arkng-thand*, *[Irkng-thand](Lore_Irkngthand.md)*, *[Arkng-thamz](Lore_Arkngthamz.md))*. The trademark features of Dwemeris are compound words, often quite long, and consonant clusters containing as many as five consonants.

Dwemer texts are written in the Dwemer Alphabet. For more information, see the main article on the [Dwemer Alphabet](https://en.uesp.net/wiki/Lore:Dwemer_Alphabet).

The only known consistent piece of Dwemeris is present on [Calcelmo's Stone](Lore_Calcelmo%27s_Stone.md), which features a text written both in the [Falmer](Lore_Falmer.md) language (which is very similar or even identical to Ayleidoon) and the Dwemer language, acting as the Elder Scrolls equivalent of the [Rosetta Stone](https://www.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rosetta_Stone). Even so, the somewhat limited knowledge of Ayleidoon was not initially enough to translate the Falmeri text accurately enough to use it to analyze the Dwemer text, but this changed when the official translation was published by Bethesda.

The Dwemer language has a possessive form similar to that seen in other elven languages. For example, "Dwemeri" means "of the Dwemer",<sup>[[9]](#cite_note-10)</sup> while "Arkthzandi" means "of [the stronghold of] Arkthzand".<sup>[[10]](#cite_note-11)</sup>

## Analysis of Calcelmo's Stone
Below is an attempt to decipher the Dwemer text from [Calcelmo's Stone](Lore_Calcelmo%27s_Stone.md) - based on the official translation given by [Kurt Kuhlmann](https://en.uesp.net/wiki/General:Kurt_Kuhlmann).<sup>[[11]](#cite_note-CST-12)</sup> Note that due to the lack of knowledge of Dwemer grammar or how exactly the compound words are constructed, it might not be very accurate.

It can be seen that the possessive pronouns are usually written together with nouns (*thua-mer* for "your people", *dua-bthar* for "our bond"). Another noticeable feature is the lack of prepositions. There also doesn't seem to be any consistent inflection.

When combined with other sources, the meanings of many words seem to be rather vague, with the actual meaning depending on the context. For example *zel* is said to mean "city"<sup>[[14]](#cite_note-KJ-15)</sup> but on Calcelmo's Stone it's translated as "under" in "...to take your people under" (which could be interpreted as "to take your people to our cities, to our domain"). In both cases the word seems to generally mean the domain of the Dwemer, or a Dwemer-inhabited area.

Similarly *arkng[d]* appears three times as "were given" (*arkngd*), "grace" (*duum-arkng*, "the grace of us" or "the grace of the Dwemer"), and "affection" (*dua-arkng*, "our affection"). It seems obvious that the word in general means giving, granting something, or the act of doing so.

*Fahlbthar* is translated as "unbind". This is more or less consistent with the word *bthar* also being translated as "allied"<sup>[[14]](#cite_note-KJ-15)</sup> or "bond" (*dua-bthar*, "our bond") while *fahl* generally seems to denote a negation ("no", "not", "un-"). Thus, *bthar* may generally mean "allied" or "bound". Compare the names of the Dwemer ruins Fahlbtharz ("not allied"? "unbound"?) on [Solstheim](Lore_Solstheim.md) and Nchu Duabthar ("symbol of our bond"?) in [High Rock](Lore_High_Rock.md).

Similarly, the name of the ruins of [Nchuand-Zel](Lore_Nchuand-Zel.md) means "radiant city"<sup>[[22]](#cite_note-ONArdanir-23)</sup>, sharing a relation with the word *nchuan*, "radiance". A similar analysis could be performed for many more ruin names.
